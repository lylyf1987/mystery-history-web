The Great Emu War: When Australia Lost to Birds

Published: February 23, 2026 • 6 min read

In 1932, Australia faced an unusual military threat. Thousands of emus—large, flightless birds native to the continent—were invading farmland in Western Australia, destroying crops and causing panic among farmers. The government's response? Deploy the Australian military with machine guns to wage war against the birds. What followed became one of the most bizarre and humorous episodes in military history: The Great Emu War.

The Emu Crisis

Following World War I, Australian veterans had been given land in Western Australia for farming. But after a promising start, things went wrong. In 1932, approximately 20,000 emus descended upon the wheat fields, drawn by abundant food and water sources. The drought-stricken birds were desperate, and they ravaged the farmers' hard-earned crops.

The farmers, struggling to recover from both the Great Depression and drought, pleaded for help. They appealed to the Minister of Defense, Sir George Pearce, requesting military assistance. Pearce, perhaps underestimating the emus, agreed to deploy soldiers armed with Lewis machine guns.

Military Operations Begin

Major G.P.W. Meredith of the Royal Australian Artillery was placed in command of the operation. He led a small team of soldiers armed with two machine guns and 10,000 rounds of ammunition. The operation began on November 2, 1932, with high hopes of quickly eliminating the emu threat.

The soldiers' first attempt was a disaster. They spotted about 50 emus and opened fire, but the birds scattered in every direction. Only a few were killed, and most escaped unharmed. The emus had quickly learned to recognize the sound of gunfire and would scatter before the soldiers could get into position.

The Emu Tactics

What the military hadn't anticipated was the emus' extraordinary tactical intelligence. These birds weren't just running randomly—they were employing sophisticated evasion strategies. Emus would split into small groups, making it impossible for machine gunners to hit more than one or two at a time.

When under direct attack, emus would run directly toward the soldiers, breaking through the defensive line at high speed. Their remarkable speed and agility made them nearly impossible to hit with slow-firing machine guns. Meredith later reported that "these birds can face machine guns with the invulnerability of tanks."

First Operation Ends in Failure

After a week of operations, the military had spent 2,500 rounds of ammunition but only killed approximately 50 emus. That's about 50 bullets per bird—an abysmal success rate. Meredith's team was recalled, and the operation was deemed a failure.

The farmers were outraged and demanded another attempt. Despite the poor results, Meredith himself defended the military's effectiveness, claiming that had his team been allowed to continue, they could have eliminated all the emus. He may have been exaggerating, but his confidence suggests the military took the operation seriously.

The Second Operation

A second operation was launched a few weeks later. This time, Meredith had learned from previous mistakes. He employed a more mobile approach, mounting one machine gun on a truck and pursuing the emus across the landscape. The tactic showed some initial success, with about 100 emus killed in the first few days.

But the emus adapted again. They began targeting the gun trucks, realizing these were the primary threat. One attack left a truck so damaged it had to be abandoned. Mechanical problems and emu damage to vehicles hampered operations, and the second operation was also suspended after limited success.

Official End of the War

By early December 1932, the Australian government had had enough. Despite spending a small fortune on ammunition and military operations, the emu population remained largely intact. The military was withdrawn, and the Great Emu War officially ended with no clear victory.

The estimated death toll varies from source to source, but most historians agree that between 200 and 1,000 emus were killed out of a population of around 20,000. That's a success rate of between 1% and 5%—hardly a military triumph.

Aftermath and Legacy

The Great Emu War became a subject of international amusement. Australian politician and humorist Sir George Pearce was mocked in parliament for authorizing what was essentially a war against birds. The operation's failure reflected poorly on the Australian military's competence.

For the emus, however, the outcome was tragic. Many were left wounded and suffering, and the population declined in the following years. The war had also damaged relations between the military and local farmers, who felt their government had both failed to help and made them the butt of international jokes.

A Deeper Lesson

Beyond its humor, the Great Emu War serves as a cautionary tale about underestimating nature and overestimating technological solutions. The emus weren't just dumb birds running randomly—they were intelligent creatures capable of adapting to threats and developing survival strategies.

The incident also raises questions about human responses to environmental challenges. When faced with natural phenomena we don't understand, our instinct is often to attack rather than adapt. The farmers' real problem was environmental and agricultural, not military. Perhaps resources would have been better spent on fencing, crop diversification, or land management practices that discouraged emu invasions.

Remembering the Great Emu War

Nearly a century later, the Great Emu War remains Australia's most peculiar military conflict. It's remembered as an example of human hubris and nature's resilience. The emus, simply trying to survive in a drought-stricken landscape, outwitted one of the world's most modern militaries.

Today, emus remain protected under Australian law, and their population has recovered. The birds still roam the Australian outback, descendants of the "enemy combatants" who defeated the Australian military. As for Meredith, he later became a respected figure in Australia, though his legacy will forever be tied to this bizarre conflict.
