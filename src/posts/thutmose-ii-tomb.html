The Missing Pharaoh: Thutmose II Tomb Discovered After 3,500 Years

The entrance to the tomb of Thutmose II, discovered in 2025. Image: Egyptian Ministry of Tourism and Antiquities

For over a century, archaeologists wondered what happened to the tomb of Thutmose II, an Egyptian pharaoh who ruled around 1493-1479 BCE. The tombs of his ancestors and descendants had been found in the Valley of the Kings, but Thutmose II's final resting place remained a mystery. In February 2025, that mystery was finally solved.

A Discovery That Changed Egyptology

A joint British-Egyptian archaeological team, led by Dr. Piers Litherland from the University of Cambridge, made the incredible discovery in the Western Wadis, about 2.45 kilometers from the famous Valley of the Kings. They had been exploring the area since 2022, when they first found a staircase leading into the cliff face.

The tomb's ceiling was painted blue with yellow stars, a signature of royal Egyptian burials.

When Dr. Litherland saw the ceiling painted blue with yellow stars, he knew immediately this was something extraordinary. This celestial design was a hallmark of royal tombs from the 18th Dynasty. The tomb had been cut directly into the rock, creating a burial chamber that had remained hidden for three and a half millennia.

🔍 First Since Tutankhamun

This is the first royal tomb discovered near the Valley of the Kings since Howard Carter found Tutankhamun's tomb in 1922 - over 100 years ago!

How They Identified the Missing Pharaoh

The tomb itself had been damaged by ancient floods, and the mummy of Thutmose II wasn't inside. In fact, his mummy had been discovered in the 19th century at a different location, where it had been moved by ancient priests to protect it from tomb robbers. So how did archaeologists know this was his tomb?

The answer lay in small fragments of alabaster found within the burial chamber. These fragments bore inscriptions that could only belong to Thutmose II. Additionally, the tomb's design and location matched what historians knew about where this "missing pharaoh" should be buried.

👑 A Powerful Family

Thutmose II was married to Queen Hatshepsut, one of ancient Egypt's most powerful female pharaohs. He was also an ancestor of the famous Tutankhamun!

Alabaster fragments with inscriptions helped identify the tomb's royal occupant.

Why This Discovery Matters

Thutmose II ruled during a crucial period in Egyptian history, the 18th Dynasty, which was one of Egypt's most prosperous eras. Finding his tomb helps archaeologists understand more about burial practices, art, and daily life during this golden age of ancient Egypt.

🏺 What's Inside?

Though the tomb was looted in ancient times, archaeologists found fragments of pottery, alabaster vessels, and pieces of the original wall decorations that survived thousands of years.

The discovery also shows how modern archaeology uses both traditional methods and new technologies. The team used careful excavation techniques combined with advanced analysis to identify and document every fragment they found.

⏳ Years of Work

The team had been searching the Western Wadis for over a decade before finding this tomb. The entrance was discovered in October 2022, but it took until 2025 to confirm the tomb's identity.

This remarkable discovery reminds us that even after centuries of exploration, the ancient world still holds secrets waiting to be uncovered. Who knows what other mysteries lie buried beneath the sands of Egypt?
