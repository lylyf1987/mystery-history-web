The Dancing Plague of 1518: When People Couldn't Stop Dancing

Published: February 23, 2026 • 8 min read

In July 1518, the city of Strasbourg in present-day France witnessed something so bizarre that it seems like medieval folklore. A woman named Frau Troffea began dancing in the street—and she didn't stop. Within a week, dozens more had joined her. By the time the phenomenon ended, approximately 400 people were dancing uncontrollably, some to the point of collapse and death. The Dancing Plague of 1518 remains one of history's most perplexing mass hysteria events.

The Outbreak Begins

Everything started quietly. Frau Troffea, a respectable citizen of Strasbourg, stepped into the street and began dancing. There was no music, no celebration, no apparent reason for her behavior. She simply could not stop.

After four days of continuous dancing, she collapsed from exhaustion. But when she recovered, she resumed dancing again. Within a week, 34 more people had joined her. Soon, the strange dancing had spread throughout the city. By August, approximately 400 people were affected, dancing day and night without rest.

Afflicted by Rhythm

Witnesses described the dancers as moving in a trance-like state. They were conscious but seemed unable to control their movements. Some danced until their feet bled. Others continued until they suffered heart attacks, strokes, or simply collapsed from exhaustion.

Historical records indicate that at least 15 people died during the plague. Modern physicians would classify these deaths as cardiovascular failure or rhabdomyolysis—muscle breakdown leading to kidney failure—but at the time, the cause was a complete mystery.

The Authorities' Response

Strasbourg's authorities initially dismissed the dancing as a form of sinful behavior. They believed the afflicted were deliberately defying religious and social norms. Local physicians consulted on the case diagnosed the condition as "hot blood" and prescribed more dancing as a cure.

The city's response was extraordinary. Rather than restraining the dancers, authorities built a wooden stage and hired musicians to play. They believed that the only way to cure the dancers was to let them dance it out—literally. The idea was that if they continued dancing without interruption, they would eventually exhaust the disease from their bodies.

Medieval Medical Theory

To understand this bizarre response, we need to understand medieval medical thinking. At the time, illness was often attributed to spiritual causes or imbalances in bodily fluids. The "hot blood" diagnosis suggested that excess heat in the blood was causing the compulsive movement.

Dancing was seen as a way to "sweat out" the excess heat and restore balance. This wasn't punishment—it was a medical treatment. The stage and musicians were provided to facilitate the cure, not to punish the afflicted.

Dancing Mania Throughout History

The Strasbourg incident wasn't unique. Between the 14th and 17th centuries, Europe experienced numerous outbreaks of dancing mania, also known as St. Vitus's Dance. Similar events occurred in Germany, Switzerland, and the Netherlands. Some outbreaks involved thousands of people.

The most famous occurred in 1374 along the Rhine River, where thousands of people danced for days, screaming, hallucinating, and collapsing from exhaustion. These events were often associated with religious pilgrimages and sometimes involved visions of saints or demons.

Religious Explanations

Contemporary explanations were deeply religious. Some believed the dancers were possessed by demons or under a curse. Others saw the dancing as divine punishment for sin. The Catholic Church sometimes attributed outbreaks to the patron saint of dancers, St. Vitus, suggesting that people had angered him.

In some cases, prayers and pilgrimages to shrines dedicated to St. Vitus were prescribed as cures. The religious nature of these explanations meant that dancing mania was seen as a spiritual crisis as much as a medical one.

Modern Explanations: Ergotism

Modern historians and scientists have proposed several explanations for the dancing plague. One popular theory is ergotism—poisoning from a fungus that grows on rye bread. Ergot contains compounds similar to LSD, which can cause hallucinations, convulsions, and strange movements.

Ergot poisoning was common in medieval Europe, especially during wet years when the fungus thrived. The symptoms of ergotism include muscle spasms, delusions, and tremors—symptoms that could be interpreted as compulsive dancing.

Mass Psychogenic Illness

Another compelling theory is mass psychogenic illness, also known as mass hysteria. This occurs when psychological distress manifests as physical symptoms in a group of people, often under stressful conditions.

In 1518, Strasbourg was experiencing severe hardship. Famine, disease, and social unrest plagued the city. The dancing may have been a manifestation of extreme stress, spreading through social contagion as people observed others dancing and subconsciously mimicked the behavior.

Cultural Triggers

Some researchers suggest that the dancing was a form of stress relief or catharsis. In medieval peasant society, dancing was associated with religious festivals and communal celebrations. During times of extreme hardship, people may have unconsciously turned to dancing as a culturally sanctioned way to express distress.

Once the dancing began, it spread through suggestion and imitation—classic mechanisms of mass hysteria. The brain's mirror neurons may have played a role, causing people to automatically mimic movements they observed in others.

The End of the Plague

The dancing plague in Strasbourg eventually subsided as mysteriously as it began. By September 1518, the dancers had stopped. Historical records don't specify exactly how it ended, but gradual exhaustion and the natural course of psychogenic illness likely played a role.

Following the plague, the city imposed strict regulations on public dancing. Religious authorities increased their emphasis on proper behavior and piety. The memory of the event lingered in local folklore for centuries.

Modern Mass Hysteria

The Dancing Plague of 1518 may seem like a medieval curiosity, but similar mass hysteria events continue to occur today. In the 1960s, laughing outbreaks affected schools in Tanzania. In the 2000s, twitching and fainting affected teenage girls in Le Roy, New York. In 2012, students at a North Carolina school experienced uncontrollable hiccupping.

These events remind us that the human mind is susceptible to collective psychological phenomena, especially under stress. The form changes—dancing, laughing, twitching—but the underlying mechanism of mass psychogenic illness remains the same.

A Lasting Mystery

More than 500 years later, the Dancing Plague of 1518 remains unexplained. Was it caused by ergot poisoning? Mass hysteria? A combination of factors? Or something else entirely—perhaps a form of neurological disorder we haven't yet identified?

What we do know is that hundreds of ordinary people found themselves compelled to dance until some of them died. The event stands as a testament to the mysterious connection between mind, body, and society—a reminder that human behavior can sometimes defy our understanding and that the line between physical and psychological illness is often blurred.
